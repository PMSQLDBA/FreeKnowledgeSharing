Cost model — the part DBAs forget

  • Compute is billed per second/hour whether or not queries run. Dev/test that runs 24×7 is the single most common waste.
  • Storage grows and rarely shrinks. Provisioned IOPS is billed even when idle.
  • Egress is the silent killer: cross-AZ, cross-region and internet traffic is charged. A chatty replication or a backup copy to another region shows up on the bill.
  • Licensing: SQL Server license-included vs bring-your-own (BYOL) changes the maths completely. Azure Hybrid Benefit is the standard answer for Azure.

Interview Q&A

Explain shared responsibility to a non-technical stakeholder.

The provider guarantees the building, power, hardware and the plumbing of the managed service. We are responsible for what we put inside it: who has access, how data is classified and encrypted, how the network is opened up, and whether backups meet our retention obligation. The clean line is: they keep the platform running, we keep the data correct, private and recoverable. If someone deletes a table, no cloud provider fixes that for us.

A team says "the cloud handles backups, we don't need a strategy." Response?

I'd agree that the platform takes backups, then ask three questions: what's the retention, where do the copies live, and when did we last restore one? Default retention is short — 7 to 35 days — and the copies sit in the same account or subscription, which means an account compromise or a mis-scoped policy takes them too. For anything under SOX or PCI I want an immutable copy in a separate account, and a restore test with a documented duration, because the restore time is our RTO whether we've measured it or not.

When would you not use a managed database service?

When a required feature isn't supported, when I need OS-level control, or when the licensing maths flips. Concretely for SQL Server: cross-database transactions with MSDTC, Filestream, custom CLR assemblies, third-party agents that must sit on the OS, or replication topologies the managed service doesn't offer. Also when the app needs a specific engine build or trace flag. If none of that applies, I take the managed service every time — the operational hours I save are worth more than the control I give up.

How do you decide between multi-AZ and multi-region?

The business RPO/RTO and the cost of the outage decide it, not my preference. Multi-AZ is cheap, near-zero RPO, and covers the failure that actually happens most — a host or a data hall. Multi-region roughly doubles the platform cost and adds egress, so I only propose it when a regional outage is a genuine business-continuity requirement, or when a regulator asks for geographic separation. My default proposal is multi-AZ for HA plus cross-region backup copies for DR, then upgrade to active replication only if the RTO demands it.

IAM — the four things that get asked

1. Policy evaluation logic

Explicit DENY anywhere → DENY (wins over everything) No applicable policy → DENY (implicit deny is the default) Explicit ALLOW and no DENY → ALLOW Chain: SCP → resource policy → identity policy → permission boundary → session policy Every layer must allow it. Any one denies → denied.

2. Roles over users

Say this"Long-lived access keys are a finding in every audit I've been part of. EC2 gets an instance profile, humans get Identity Center, applications get a role — nothing carries a static key."

3. Identity policy vs resource policy

# Identity policy: attached to a role — "this principal may do X"
{
  "Version": "2012-10-17",
  "Statement": [{
    "Sid": "ReadBackupBucket",
    "Effect": "Allow",
    "Action": ["s3:GetObject","s3:ListBucket"],
    "Resource": ["arn:aws:s3:::sql-backups-prod","arn:aws:s3:::sql-backups-prod/*"],
    "Condition": {"Bool": {"aws:SecureTransport": "true"}}
  }]
}

# Resource policy: attached to the S3 bucket — "these principals may touch me"
{
  "Effect": "Deny",
  "Principal": "*",
  "Action": "s3:*",
  "Resource": "arn:aws:s3:::sql-backups-prod/*",
  "Condition": {"Bool": {"aws:SecureTransport": "false"}}
}

Cross-account access needs both sides: the resource policy trusts the other account, and the identity policy in that account allows the call.

4. Permission boundaries

A boundary is a maximum, not a grant. Give a team lead the ability to create roles, but bound those roles so they can never exceed what you allow. This is how you delegate without handing over the keys.

Interview Q&A

How is an SCP different from an IAM policy?

An SCP is a guardrail at the account or OU level — it sets the maximum permissions available in that account and grants nothing on its own. An IAM policy grants. The practical difference: if the SCP denies rds:DeleteDBInstance, then even the account root user cannot delete an RDS instance, no matter what IAM says. That's why I put destructive-action denies in SCPs for production data accounts rather than trusting per-role policies that someone can edit.

An EC2 instance running SQL Server needs to read backups from S3. How do you grant it?

Instance profile with an IAM role — never an access key on the box. The role gets a scoped policy: s3:GetObject and s3:ListBucket on that one bucket prefix, with a condition requiring TLS. The bucket has a bucket policy denying non-TLS and denying principals outside the account, plus SSE-KMS, and the KMS key policy grants decrypt to that role. Credentials rotate automatically via the metadata service, so there's nothing to leak and nothing to rotate manually.

Walk me through IAM policy evaluation.

Start from implicit deny — nothing is allowed unless something allows it. Then an explicit deny anywhere in the chain wins, full stop. The chain is: service control policy at the org level, then the resource-based policy, then the identity policy on the principal, then any permission boundary, then a session policy if the credentials came from assume-role. Every layer that applies must allow the action. When someone says "my policy says allow but I get denied", it's almost always an SCP, a boundary, or a missing resource-policy trust on the other account.

Why multiple accounts instead of one account with tags?

Because the account is the only hard boundary AWS gives you. Tags are advisory — a mistyped condition and a policy is bypassed. Separate accounts give a clean blast radius, separate quotas, separate billing without allocation guesswork, and an SCP ceiling you can't accidentally over-grant past. For a regulated estate I'd put production data in its own account, with the log archive in a third account that even production admins can't write to. That's what makes backup and audit evidence credible to an auditor.

Troubleshooting "can't connect to the database"

  1. DNS — does the endpoint resolve? From inside the VPC it should resolve to a private IP.
  2. Security group — inbound 1433/5432/3306 from the client's SG?
  3. NACL — both inbound and outbound ephemeral ports (1024–65535)?
  4. Route table — is there a path at all? Peering/TGW routes on both sides?
  5. Subnet — is the client in a subnet that can reach the data subnet?
  6. Instance state — RDS status, is it in a failover?

Cost traps a DBA should know

  • Cross-AZ traffic is charged both ways. A chatty app in AZ-a hitting a database in AZ-b pays for every byte. Multi-AZ RDS replication traffic itself is free, but your app traffic is not.
  • NAT Gateway per GB — if backups or patches egress through NAT, use a gateway endpoint for S3 instead and the cost disappears.
  • Interface endpoints are billed hourly per AZ. Ten endpoints × three AZs adds up; consolidate what you actually use.

Interview Q&A

Security group vs NACL — when do you reach for each?

Security groups are stateful and attach to the instance's network interface, so I allow inbound 1433 from the app's security group and the return traffic is handled automatically. That's where 95% of my access control lives, because referencing a security group instead of a CIDR keeps the rule meaningful as the estate changes. NACLs are stateless, subnet-wide and support explicit deny — I use them as a coarse guardrail, for example denying a compromised CIDR quickly, or making sure the data subnet can never talk to the internet. If someone's connection works one way and hangs the other, my first thought is a NACL missing the ephemeral port range.

Your RDS instance is in a private subnet and needs to write backups to S3. How?

A gateway VPC endpoint for S3, with a route-table entry in the data subnets. It keeps the traffic on the AWS network, needs no internet gateway or NAT, and it's free — which matters because backup egress through a NAT gateway is billed per gigabyte and gets expensive fast on a large estate. I'd also add an endpoint policy restricting which buckets can be reached through it, so a compromised host can't exfiltrate to an arbitrary bucket.

On-prem SQL Server needs to replicate to AWS for a migration. What network design?

Direct Connect as the primary path for predictable bandwidth and latency, with a Site-to-Site VPN as a backup — DX alone is a single point of failure and the VPN failover is cheap insurance. I'd terminate on a Transit Gateway so future accounts get connectivity without new tunnels, and check for CIDR overlap with the on-prem range before anything else, because that's the problem you can't fix later. Then I'd measure actual throughput with a test transfer before promising a cutover window, since the migration plan lives or dies on how long the initial full load takes.

What is PrivateLink and when do you use it?

PrivateLink exposes a service through an interface endpoint — an ENI with a private IP inside my VPC — rather than routing between networks. Two reasons I use it: reaching AWS APIs like KMS or Secrets Manager without any internet path, and consuming a service from another VPC or a vendor without peering, which avoids CIDR overlap problems and stops me from sharing routes with a network I don't control. The trade-off is that interface endpoints are billed hourly per AZ plus data, so I only create the ones actually in use.

Interview Q&A

Which instance family for SQL Server and why?

R family by default. SQL Server performance is driven by how much of the working set fits in the buffer pool, and R gives roughly 8 GB per vCPU versus 4 on M. That matters twice: it hits the memory target, and because SQL Server is licensed per core, reaching that memory with fewer cores directly cuts the licence bill — which is usually larger than the compute bill. I'd use i4i if tempdb is the bottleneck and I can rebuild it on the ephemeral NVMe at startup, and I stay away from T instances for anything production because credit exhaustion produces a performance cliff that looks like a database problem and isn't.

Why can't you just put a database in an Auto Scaling group?

Because an ASG's remediation is to terminate and replace, and terminating a stateful primary destroys data or forces an unplanned failover. ASGs assume nodes are interchangeable and disposable; databases have identity, local state and a quorum. For the database tier I use RDS Multi-AZ, or on EC2 a Failover Cluster Instance or an Always On AG where failover is coordinated and the data lives on shared or replicated storage. The one thing I might put in an ASG is a read-replica fleet behind a load balancer, and even then only if the app can tolerate a replica disappearing mid-query.

A Lambda function connects to RDS and you start seeing connection errors under load. Diagnosis?

Connection exhaustion from concurrency. Each Lambda invocation is its own execution environment, so a thousand concurrent invocations means a thousand connection attempts, and the database's max connections is nowhere near that. The fix is RDS Proxy in front — it pools and multiplexes so the database sees a stable number of connections — plus a reserved concurrency limit on the function as a hard ceiling. I'd also check the function isn't opening the connection inside the handler instead of outside it, which prevents reuse across warm invocations.

How do you patch 40 SQL Server EC2 instances?

SSM Patch Manager with patch baselines and maintenance windows, targeted by tag rather than by instance ID so new servers are covered automatically. Non-prod patches first on a baseline with a short approval delay; production follows a week later after the non-prod soak. For the database itself I stage cumulative updates the same way — snapshot or AMI first, patch the secondary replica, fail over, patch the old primary. Nobody RDPs to a box to click Next. The reporting side matters as much as the patching: compliance reports out of SSM are what I hand to audit.

Interview Q&A

Design storage for a 2 TB SQL Server on EC2 with heavy write load.

Separate volumes, never one big disk. OS on a small gp3. Data on gp3 sized for the working set with IOPS provisioned to measured demand — I'd baseline from the on-prem perfmon rather than guess. The log gets io2 Block Express because log writes are sequential and latency-critical, and WRITELOG waits are the thing that actually stalls a write-heavy OLTP system. TempDB on instance-store NVMe if I'm on an i-family instance, with a startup script to recreate the folders since it's wiped on stop. Backups to st1 then lifecycled to S3. NTFS formatted at 64 KB allocation unit, EBS-optimised instance, and Lock Pages in Memory set.

Why gp3 over gp2?

gp2 ties performance to capacity — 3 IOPS per GB — so the only way to get 12,000 IOPS is to buy 4 TB whether you need the space or not. gp3 gives a 3,000 IOPS and 125 MB/s baseline at any size and lets you provision IOPS and throughput independently. It's usually cheaper for the same performance, and the change is an online volume modification with no downtime. When I inherit an estate, migrating gp2 to gp3 is one of the fastest cost wins available, and it needs no application change.

How do you protect backups from ransomware?

Three layers. First, S3 Object Lock or Backup Vault Lock in compliance mode so the objects genuinely cannot be deleted before retention expires — not even by root, which is the whole point. Second, the copy lives in a different account that the production admins have no path into, so a compromise of production credentials doesn't reach the vault. Third, versioning plus MFA delete on the bucket, and an SCP denying lifecycle-policy changes. Then I test the restore, because an immutable backup you've never restored is a guess. That's the answer that satisfies both the security team and the auditor.

Snapshot or native backup?

Both, for different jobs. Snapshots give me a fast whole-instance recovery and they're cheap to schedule, but restoring one database out of an instance means restoring the whole thing to a new instance first — which is slow when someone just dropped a table. Native backups give me database-level and even page-level restores, a portable file I can move anywhere including back on-prem, and CHECKSUM plus RESTORE VERIFYONLY as evidence the backup is readable. On RDS I lean on automated backups and PITR, but I still take native backups to S3 for anything with a long retention requirement or a chance of leaving the platform.

Interview Q&A

RDS or EC2 for SQL Server? Walk me through the decision.

RDS by default, and I need a reason to leave it. The reasons that actually come up: I need sysadmin — for a third-party product, a custom CLR assembly, or an agent that must run on the OS. FILESTREAM. Self-managed Always On AGs with a specific topology. Cross-instance MSDTC. A database over 16 TB. Or a licensing arrangement that only works BYOL on a dedicated host. If none of those apply, RDS wins because patching, backups, Multi-AZ failover and monitoring stop being my weekend. When they do apply, EC2 — or RDS Custom if I need OS access but still want managed backups and Multi-AZ.

How does RDS Multi-AZ failover work and what's the RTO?

AWS keeps a synchronous standby in a second AZ. On failure — or on a patch, or a manual reboot with failover — the endpoint's DNS record is repointed to the standby. Typically 60 to 120 seconds for SQL Server. RPO is zero because the commit is synchronous. Two things I'd flag to an application team: the standby isn't readable, so it's not a reporting solution, and the DNS flip means the app must handle a reconnect — connection pools that cache the resolved IP will keep failing until they're recycled, so I want a short DNS TTL respected and retry logic in the connection string. And I test the failover on a schedule; an untested failover is a hypothesis.

You need 7-year backup retention on RDS. Automated backups max out at 35 days. Solve it.

Automated backups handle operational recovery — 35 days of PITR. For the retention obligation I use AWS Backup with a plan that copies snapshots into a vault in a separate account, with Vault Lock set so the retention is immutable and the production admins have no delete path. In parallel, for anything that might need a granular or portable restore, native backup to S3 with rds_backup_database, lifecycled to Glacier Deep Archive with Object Lock. Then the part people skip: a quarterly restore test with the duration recorded, because a seven-year-old backup nobody has ever restored is not a control, it's a story.

RDS storage is nearly full. What are your options?

Grow it — modify allocated storage, which is an online operation, and enable storage autoscaling with a sensible maximum so it doesn't happen at 2 a.m. But the critical caveat: RDS storage cannot be shrunk. Once it grows, that's the floor of your bill forever unless you migrate to a new smaller instance via dump-and-load or DMS. So before growing, I check what's actually consuming it — a runaway log because of a long-running transaction or a broken log backup chain, tempdb, or a table nobody archived. And there's a 6-hour cooldown between storage modifications, so I size the growth generously rather than nibbling at it.

Which SQL Server features force you off RDS?

The ones I check first in any assessment: FILESTREAM and FileTable, anything needing sysadmin, self-managed Always On AGs, Log Shipping, Machine Learning Services, Data Quality Services, Policy-Based Management, SSAS, and xp_cmdshell. Then the softer ones — linked servers to on-prem with Kerberos, MSDTC across instances, custom certificates, and third-party agents. I run the DMA against the source and cross-reference the findings against the RDS unsupported list; anything left goes on a decision log with an owner. That document is what stops a migration getting halfway and discovering an app needs FILESTREAM.

Interview Q&A

What makes Aurora different from RDS?

The storage layer. RDS is a database instance with an EBS volume attached and, for Multi-AZ, a second copy being written to. Aurora separates compute from a distributed storage service that keeps six copies across three availability zones and acknowledges a write on a four-of-six quorum. Because all the replicas read from the same storage, adding a reader doesn't copy data, replica lag is typically under 100 milliseconds, and failover is around 30 seconds because there's nothing to catch up. Storage grows automatically to 128 TB. The catch for a SQL Server DBA is that Aurora is MySQL or PostgreSQL compatible only — it's a conversion target, not a lift-and-shift destination.

When is DynamoDB the wrong choice?

When you can't enumerate the access patterns in advance, or when the business needs ad-hoc analytical queries and joins. DynamoDB rewards a data model built backwards from a fixed set of queries — get order by ID, list orders for a customer by date. The moment someone says "and we'd like to slice this by any dimension", you're either running Scans, which cost real money and don't scale, or bolting on a second system. It's also the wrong choice if the relational integrity is genuinely complex, because you'll end up implementing transactions and constraints in application code and doing it worse than a database would.

Explain Aurora Serverless v2 and when you'd use it.

It scales the writer and readers in-place in 0.5 ACU increments, up and down, without dropping connections — which is what made v1 unusable for many workloads, since v1 needed a quiet moment to scale and killed sessions. I'd use it for workloads with genuinely variable or unpredictable demand: dev and test that idles overnight, a reporting replica that spikes at month-end, or a new application where I have no idea what the steady state will be. For a flat, predictable production OLTP load I'd still use provisioned instances with a reservation, because the serverless premium per ACU only pays off if you're actually idling.

A partition key choice — walk me through it.

The partition key decides which physical partition an item lands on, so the only requirement that matters is even distribution across the key space, combined with the access pattern. If I pick something like a status field with four values, every write for "PENDING" hits one partition and I get throttled while the table sits at 1% of its provisioned capacity — the classic hot-key problem. So I want high cardinality: customer ID, order ID, tenant ID. If the natural key is skewed, I add a write-sharding suffix and fan out reads across the shards. And I model the sort key to serve the range queries — a date prefix gives me "orders for this customer in July" for free.

The near-zero-downtime cutover runbook

  1. Assess — DMA + Fleet Advisor. Log every blocker with an owner.
  2. Build the target — RDS/Aurora, sized from measured on-prem load, not from the current VM spec.
  3. Schema first — SCT for heterogeneous; native backup/restore or scripted schema for homogeneous. Indexes/FKs off.
  4. Full load — measure it. This number sets the plan.
  5. Rebuild indexes and constraints, update statistics.
  6. CDC on — let it run for days. Watch CDCLatencySource and CDCLatencyTarget.
  7. Validate — DMS data validation, row counts, checksums, and the business's own reconciliation queries.
  8. Rehearse — a full cutover rehearsal on a clone. Time it. Rehearse the rollback too.
  9. Cutover — quiesce writes, wait for CDC latency 0, run the reconciliation, repoint the app (DNS/CNAME), enable jobs, smoke test.
  10. Rollback path stays open — until sign-off, the source stays intact and reverse replication is running if the RPO demands it.

Interview Q&A

Migrate a 4 TB SQL Server database to AWS with under 15 minutes of downtime. How?

The downtime budget rules out backup-restore over the wire, so it's a replicated cutover. If the target is RDS for SQL Server, I'd take a full native backup to S3, restore it to the target, then use DMS in CDC-only mode from that point — or run log shipping to an EC2 target if I need a self-managed instance. Either way the sequence is: seed the target from a backup, catch up with changes, let it run for days while I validate. The cutover itself is: stop the app writes, wait for replication latency to hit zero, run the reconciliation counts, repoint the connection string via DNS, start the Agent jobs, smoke test. That's minutes, not hours, because all the heavy lifting happened while the source was live. And I rehearse the whole thing on a clone first, including the rollback.

SQL Server to Aurora PostgreSQL. What breaks?

More than people expect. T-SQL procedures and functions need conversion, and SCT will get maybe 70–80% automatically — the rest is manual, especially anything with temp tables, cursors, or dynamic SQL. Data types: DATETIME2, MONEY, UNIQUEIDENTIFIER, VARBINARY(MAX) all need decisions. Identity columns become sequences with different semantics. Case sensitivity and collation catch teams out — SQL Server is usually case-insensitive, PostgreSQL is not, so queries that worked start returning nothing. SQL Agent jobs have no equivalent and become Lambda or pg_cron. And the application's SQL itself needs review, because ORMs generate dialect-specific SQL. I'd scope the application remediation as the largest line item, not the data movement.

DMS full load is running for 30 hours. What do you check?

First, whether the target still has its secondary indexes and constraints on — that's the usual answer, because every insert maintains every index. Drop them, load, rebuild. Then: parallel load settings, since MaxFullLoadSubTasks defaults conservatively and table-level parallelism with partition-based ranges helps on the big tables. Then the replication instance size — full load is CPU and network bound, and a t3 instance will never finish. Then the network path: is it going over the internet or Direct Connect, and what's the measured throughput. And on the target, LOB handling — full LOB mode is dramatically slower, so I'd set a limited LOB size if the data allows it. If it's still slow, I'd split the task by table group and run several in parallel.

How do you prove the migration was correct?

Three layers, and I need all three before anyone signs. DMS validation runs its own row-by-row comparison and reports mismatches per table — that's the mechanical check. Then my own reconciliation: row counts per table, checksums or hash aggregates on the key columns, and min/max on the identity or date ranges. Then the business check, which is the one that matters: the finance team runs their month-end report against both and the numbers match. I capture all of it as evidence with timestamps, because for a SOX-scoped system the migration itself becomes an audit artifact. And I keep the source read-only rather than deleting it until that sign-off is in writing.

Interview Q&A

Explain envelope encryption.

The master key never leaves KMS's HSM, so we don't encrypt terabytes with it — that would mean shipping the data to KMS. Instead the service asks KMS to generate a data key; KMS returns it twice, once in plaintext and once encrypted under the master key. The service encrypts the data with the plaintext key, throws that key away, and stores the encrypted copy next to the data. To read, it sends the encrypted data key back to KMS, which decrypts it and returns the plaintext key. Two things fall out of that design and both matter in an audit: every decrypt is a KMS API call logged in CloudTrail, and revoking access to the master key instantly renders the data unreadable without touching the data itself.

Why a customer-managed key over the AWS-managed one?

Control and evidence. With aws/rds I can't edit the key policy, I can't set my own rotation schedule, and I can't share it cross-account — which means I can't restore an encrypted snapshot into a different account, and that's exactly what a cross-account backup vault needs. A customer-managed key gives me a key policy I own, so I can deny decrypt to everyone except the specific roles that need it, enable rotation on my schedule, and grant another account access deliberately. It also gives the auditor a clean answer to "who can decrypt this data" — it's readable in one policy document rather than implied.

An existing RDS instance isn't encrypted. Production. What do you do?

There's no in-place path — you can't encrypt an existing instance and you can't change the key later either, which is why this is a design decision, not an operational one. The route is: take a snapshot, copy the snapshot while specifying the KMS key (the copy operation is where encryption gets applied), restore that copy to a new instance, then cut over. So it needs a maintenance window and, if the database is large, that window is not short. To minimise it, I'd restore the encrypted copy ahead of time and use DMS CDC to catch it up, then the actual cutover is a connection-string change. And I'd add a Config rule plus an SCP afterwards so nobody creates an unencrypted instance again.

How do you produce audit evidence for a PCI assessment on AWS?

I don't produce it at assessment time — I build the estate so the evidence is a query. CloudTrail organisation trail into a log-archive account nobody has delete rights in, with log file validation on and Object Lock on the bucket. AWS Config recording everything, with rules mapping to the requirements — encryption on, public access blocked, no 0.0.0.0/0 on port 1433. Security Hub with the PCI-DSS standard enabled aggregates the findings across accounts into one dashboard, and Audit Manager can package it. Then the manual pieces: access reviews from IAM last-accessed data, and the restore test records. When the assessor asks "prove the database was encrypted for the whole period", the answer is a Config timeline, not a screenshot.

Interview Q&A

What do you monitor on an RDS instance and why those?

Free storage space first, because a full disk is the most common RDS incident and it takes the database down hard — I alarm at 15% with the growth trend, not just the level. Then DB load from Performance Insights measured against the vCPU count, because that tells me whether sessions are queueing rather than just whether CPU looks busy. Read and write latency for the storage story, connections against the maximum to catch leaks and Lambda storms, replica lag against the stated RPO, and burst balance if anything's still on gp2. Plus an RDS event subscription for failovers and maintenance, because I should know a failover happened before the application team tells me.

The database was slow for 20 minutes at 3 a.m. and is fine now. How do you find out what happened?

Performance Insights first — it retains the history, so I set the window to that 20 minutes and look at DB load sliced by wait type. If it's IO, I cross-check read latency and IOPS in CloudWatch against the volume's provisioned limits and burst balance. If it's locks, top SQL will show me the blocker. Then I check what else was running: was it inside the maintenance window, did a backup or a heavy Agent job overlap, did an Auto Scaling event add load? CloudTrail tells me whether anyone changed anything. If Query Store is on, I compare the plan for that window against the day before. The point is that the evidence exists after the fact — I don't need to have been awake for it.

How would you cut a bloated AWS database bill?

In order of effort-to-saving. First the free money: non-production stopped outside business hours on a schedule, orphaned snapshots and unattached volumes deleted, gp2 moved to gp3. Then S3 lifecycle so old backups fall into Glacier instead of sitting in Standard for years. Then right-sizing with Compute Optimizer and Performance Insights as the evidence — most inherited estates have instances sized from a fear-based on-prem spec. Only then Savings Plans, because committing to the wrong size for three years locks in the waste. And throughout, tag enforcement so I can actually attribute cost per application, because the conversation with an application owner goes very differently when I can show them their number.

Interview Q&A

Design DR for a business-critical SQL Server estate on AWS. RPO 5 minutes, RTO 1 hour.

Those numbers point straight at pilot light, not multi-site. In-region HA is RDS Multi-AZ or, on EC2, an Always On AG with a sync replica in a second AZ — that covers the failure that actually happens. For the region, an async replica or log shipping into the DR region keeps me inside a five-minute RPO with room to spare, and the app tier is AMIs plus infrastructure-as-code with the Auto Scaling group at zero, which is what makes a one-hour RTO affordable. Route 53 failover records with health checks, TTL at 60 seconds. Then the parts that make it real: cross-region backup copies into a locked vault for the logical-corruption case that replication won't save me from, and a documented drill twice a year where we actually promote and time it. An untested DR plan is a slide, not a control.

What's special about running an Always On AG on EC2 versus on-prem?

Networking, mostly. Each availability zone is a different subnet, so the WSFC cluster and the AG listener need an IP address in each subnet with an OR dependency — get that wrong and the listener only comes online in one AZ. Clients need MultiSubnetFailover=True and the DNS RegisterAllProvidersIP setting, or they'll sit through a DNS timeout on every failover. Quorum needs a witness placed in a third AZ, because a two-node cluster without one dies on any single failure. And the storage isn't shared, so a Failover Cluster Instance needs FSx for Windows File Server rather than a SAN. None of it is hard, but every one of those is a real outage if it's missed.

Your RTO is 30 minutes but a restore takes 4 hours. What do you say to the business?

I tell them plainly that we currently have a four-hour RTO, not a thirty-minute one, and that the gap has been there since before anyone measured it. Then I put options with prices next to them: a warm standby with a promoted replica gets us to minutes but costs roughly another full environment; splitting the database so the critical 10% restores first gets us most of the way for engineering effort; or the business accepts four hours and we document it. What I don't do is agree to a number I can't hit — the point of measuring the restore is to make the conversation honest. Then whatever they choose, we test it and I report the measured time each quarter.

Multi-AZ, cross-region replica, and backups. Which one saves you from ransomware?

Only the backups, and only if they're immutable and in a separate account. Multi-AZ replicates the encryption instantly. A cross-region replica does the same, just further away. That's the whole point of the distinction between availability and recoverability: replication protects against infrastructure failure and faithfully copies everything else, including a malicious or accidental change. So the design needs a backup copy with Object Lock or Vault Lock in compliance mode, held in an account the production credentials cannot reach, with a tested restore. That's the answer, and the reason I always separate the two words rather than saying "we're covered".

Interview Q&A

Explain the Azure hierarchy and where you'd enforce "no public database endpoints".

Tenant at the top for identity, then management groups, subscriptions, resource groups, resources — and both RBAC and Policy inherit downward. That rule belongs in Azure Policy at a management group, not on individual resources, because a policy at the corp management group applies to every subscription under it including ones created next year. I'd use a Deny effect so non-compliant deployments fail at the request, plus an Audit policy alongside so I get the compliance dashboard showing historical state. Putting it on the resource group would mean the next team who creates their own resource group is unprotected, which is exactly how these gaps happen.

RBAC or Policy — which one stops someone deleting a database?

Neither, cleanly, which is why I use both plus a lock. RBAC controls who can call the delete — so the DBA group gets SQL DB Contributor and nobody gets Owner on production. Policy controls what can exist and is better at prevention of misconfiguration than of deletion. For deletion specifically I add a CanNotDelete resource lock, which blocks the call regardless of RBAC — including for an Owner — until someone deliberately removes the lock, and that removal is itself logged in the Activity Log. Three layers, each catching what the others miss.

What is a managed identity and why does it matter to a DBA?

It's a service principal whose credential Azure creates, rotates and hands out — the resource asks the platform for a token and never sees a secret. It matters because it removes the single worst pattern in database operations: a password in a connection string, a config file, or a scheduled task. My SQL VM gets a managed identity, that identity is granted Key Vault Secrets User on the vault holding the TDE key, and the backup job authenticates with a token. Nothing to store, nothing to rotate, nothing to leak, and the access is visible as a role assignment rather than as tribal knowledge.

Compare an AWS account to an Azure subscription.

People map them one-to-one and it's misleading. An AWS account is a hard isolation boundary — separate IAM, and even root is capped by an SCP from the organisation. An Azure subscription is primarily a billing and quota boundary; the real security boundary is the tenant, because a Global Administrator can elevate across every subscription in it. Practically that means in AWS I get isolation by creating accounts, whereas in Azure I get it from management groups plus Policy plus disciplined RBAC, and I have to think harder about who holds tenant-level roles. The equivalent of an SCP is Azure Policy with a Deny effect, but it's not identical — Policy governs resource properties, an SCP governs API actions.

Interview Q&A

Private endpoint or service endpoint for Azure SQL?

Private endpoint, essentially always, for a production database. A service endpoint just keeps the traffic on Microsoft's backbone — the SQL server still has a public IP and a public DNS name, and access control is a VNet rule on the firewall. A private endpoint gives the database an actual NIC with a private IP inside my VNet, so I can disable public network access entirely, and on-prem clients can reach it over ExpressRoute, which service endpoints can't do at all. The cost is a few pounds a month and one piece of homework: the private DNS zone must be linked to every VNet that needs to resolve it, and on-prem DNS needs a conditional forwarder. That DNS step is where every private endpoint deployment goes wrong.

Two spokes can't talk to each other despite both being peered to the hub. Why?

Peering isn't transitive. Spoke A peers to the hub and spoke B peers to the hub, but that doesn't create a path from A to B — Azure won't route through the hub just because it's in the middle. You need a user-defined route in each spoke sending the other spoke's range to the hub's firewall or NVA as the next hop, and the firewall needs a rule allowing it. If on-prem is also involved, the hub peering needs "allow gateway transit" and the spoke needs "use remote gateway". I'd verify with the effective route table on the NIC rather than reading the config, because that shows what Azure will actually do.

How do you give an on-prem application access to Azure SQL Managed Instance?

MI already lives in your VNet with a private IP, so the connectivity is ExpressRoute or a site-to-site VPN into the hub, peering to the MI spoke, and a route. Three things then bite. First, DNS: on-prem needs a conditional forwarder for database.windows.net to the Azure DNS resolver, or the name won't resolve to the private address. Second, port 1433 for the private endpoint but 3342 if they're using the public endpoint, and the MI subnet's NSG and route table have required rules you must not break — MI manages that subnet. Third, if they want Windows authentication, that needs Entra Domain Services or a domain controller reachable from the MI subnet. I'd test with a connectivity check from an on-prem host before anyone schedules a cutover.

Compare an NSG to an AWS security group.

Both are stateful, which is the important similarity — return traffic is automatic in both, unlike an AWS NACL. The differences: an NSG has explicit deny rules and numeric priorities where a security group is allow-only and unordered; an NSG can attach to a subnet as well as a NIC, and both sets apply, which produces surprises when someone adds a subnet NSG later; and instead of referencing another security group I reference an Application Security Group, which is a tag on the NICs. Azure also gives service tags — Sql, AzureBackup, Storage — which are maintained IP ranges I don't have to track. Functionally they solve the same problem, but the effective-rules view in Azure is essential because of the subnet-plus-NIC combination.

Interview Q&A

Host caching settings for a SQL Server VM. Go.

ReadOnly on the data disks, None on the log disk. The data disks benefit because reads get served from the host's local SSD cache for free, and SQL Server's own buffer pool plus that cache means a lot of reads never touch remote storage. The log gets None because write-back caching sits between SQL Server and durable storage, and the write-ahead logging guarantee depends on a log write being genuinely hardened before the commit is acknowledged. TempDB I'd put on the VM's local ephemeral disk where the series has one, which is faster and free, with a startup task to recreate the folder because it's wiped on deallocation.

How do you cut SQL Server licence cost on Azure VMs?

Three levers, in order of size. Azure Hybrid Benefit — if we already own Software Assurance, we bring the licence and stop paying the per-hour SQL premium, and with the SQL IaaS extension registered I can switch licence type on a running VM without redeploying. Constrained vCPU sizing — an E64 with 16 active vCPUs keeps the full memory and IO envelope but licenses 16 cores, which is the single biggest win on memory-hungry, low-CPU workloads. And then the honest one: right-size, because a lot of the estate was specced from a nervous on-prem sizing exercise years ago. I'd take the evidence from actual CPU and memory telemetry rather than argue from opinion.

Design storage for a SQL Server VM in Azure.

Premium SSD v2 as the default, because like gp3 it decouples capacity from IOPS and throughput, so I size each dimension to measured demand rather than buying 4 TB to get the IOPS. Separate disks: OS, data with ReadOnly caching, log with no caching — Ultra Disk if the log latency requirement is genuinely sub-millisecond — tempdb on the local NVMe, and backups going straight to blob rather than a disk. Stripe multiple data disks with Storage Spaces if I need more IOPS than one disk delivers, 64 KB allocation unit, and let the SQL IaaS extension apply the storage configuration at deploy so it's consistent across the fleet rather than dependent on whoever built the box.

Interview Q&A

SQL Database, Managed Instance or SQL on VM — how do you choose?

I start from what the application needs, not from what's newest. If it's an existing on-prem SQL Server, Managed Instance is my default target because it keeps the instance-level surface: SQL Agent jobs, cross-database queries, linked servers, Service Broker, native RESTORE from a backup file. Most applications migrate without code changes, which is where the real cost lives. Azure SQL Database is right for new applications, or ones already scoped to a single database, and it gives me Hyperscale and serverless options MI can't match. SQL on VM is when something forces it — FILESTREAM, a third-party agent on the OS, a specific version, or a licensing arrangement. The question I ask the application team is "do you use Agent jobs and do you query across databases", and the answer usually decides it in one minute.

General Purpose vs Business Critical. What actually differs?

The storage and HA architecture. General Purpose separates compute from remote premium storage — a failure means spinning up a new compute node and reattaching the files, so failover takes a few minutes and IO latency is a network hop. Business Critical puts the data on local SSD and runs a four-node Always On availability group underneath, so failover is seconds, IO latency is sub-millisecond, and you get a readable secondary at no extra cost plus In-Memory OLTP. It's roughly two and a half times the price. My rule: General Purpose unless the workload has proven latency sensitivity, needs the read scale-out, or the RTO doesn't tolerate a few minutes. And I'd add zone redundancy to either, which is nearly free protection against a zone failure.

Migrate a 3 TB SQL Server 2019 instance to MI with minimal downtime. How?

Managed Instance link. It sets up a distributed availability group from the on-prem instance to MI, so the seeding and the ongoing replication happen while the source is fully live. That gives me three things a backup-restore can't: I can leave it replicating for weeks, I can use the MI side as a readable secondary to test the application against real data before committing, and the cutover is a controlled failover measured in seconds rather than a restore measured in hours. Prerequisites are SQL Server 2016 or later with the right CU, network line-of-sight to the MI subnet, and certificate-based trust. I'd still run DMA first for feature blockers, rehearse the failover, and keep the link so there's a path back until sign-off. If the link weren't available, the fallback is a native backup to blob, RESTORE FROM URL on MI, then DMS in CDC mode to catch up.

There's no SQL Agent in Azure SQL Database. How do you schedule jobs?

Elastic Jobs is the native answer — it targets a group of databases, runs T-SQL on a schedule, and is designed for the fan-out case across a pool. For anything beyond T-SQL, Azure Automation runbooks or a Logic App, and for event-driven work a Function with a timer trigger. The honest framing in an interview: this is one of the concrete reasons an existing estate goes to Managed Instance instead, because MI has a real SQL Agent and a portfolio of Agent jobs is often days of rewriting. If the target is SQL Database anyway, I'd inventory the jobs first — most of them are index maintenance and backups that the platform now does for me, so the list is usually shorter than people fear.

Auto-failover group or active geo-replication?

Failover group in almost every case, because it wraps geo-replication and adds a listener endpoint that survives the failover — so the application's connection string never changes and I'm not coordinating a config push during an incident. It also fails over a group of databases together, which matters when an application spans several, and it supports Managed Instance where geo-replication doesn't. Geo-replication on its own is for read scale-out to a specific region, or when I want up to four secondaries and manual control. Both give sub-five-second RPO. The thing I'd flag: the automatic failover has an hour's grace period by default, deliberately, so Microsoft doesn't fail you over for a transient blip — if the RTO is tighter, the failover is a decision someone has to make, and that person needs a runbook.

Interview Q&A

Which Cosmos DB consistency level would you pick and why?

Session, unless something specific overrides it. Session gives each client read-your-own-writes, which is what people almost always mean when they ask for strong consistency — they've noticed a user updating a profile and not seeing the change. Strong is linearizable across regions and costs roughly double the RUs on reads while restricting you to single-region writes, so it buys a guarantee most applications never needed. Bounded staleness is the interesting middle ground when there's a real business tolerance expressed in seconds. The framing I'd give a team is that consistency, latency, availability and cost are one dial in Cosmos, and I want them to justify moving it rather than defaulting to the most expensive setting out of nervousness.

Migrate 200 SSIS packages to Azure. What's your plan?

Split the migration from the rewrite, because trying to do both at once is how these programmes stall. First, lift them onto the Azure-SSIS integration runtime in Data Factory — the packages run essentially as-is, the SSISDB catalogue moves to a Managed Instance or SQL Database, and the estate is off the on-prem servers. That gets the infrastructure win banked. Then, over the following quarters, rewrite the packages as Data Factory or Fabric pipelines in priority order, retiring the SSIS IR when the last one moves. Along the way a chunk of them turn out to be unused or duplicated, so the real number is always smaller than 200. What I wouldn't do is promise a rewrite as part of the migration timeline.

On-prem SQL Server needs to feed an Azure data warehouse nightly. Design it.

Data Factory with a self-hosted integration runtime on-prem — that's the only component that can reach a private SQL Server, and it makes an outbound connection, so no inbound firewall hole. It authenticates to Azure with a managed identity where possible, pulls incrementally using a watermark column or change tracking rather than a full extract, stages to blob or ADLS in Parquet, then loads into the warehouse. I'd add: high-availability for the self-hosted IR by registering a second node, because a single IR node is a single point of failure everyone forgets, and monitoring on the pipeline with alerts to the on-call channel rather than an email nobody reads.

Interview Q&A

Which Azure tool for which migration? Give me the map.

Azure Migrate for assessment and the business case — it's what gives me an evidence-based SKU recommendation from real telemetry rather than a guess. DMA for the compatibility findings on a specific database, which is where I get the list of blockers that decides MI versus VM. Then the migration itself: Managed Instance link if the target is MI and downtime matters, because it's a distributed availability group and the cutover is seconds; Azure DMS online if the link isn't available or the target is SQL Database; backup to URL and restore from URL if there's an honest maintenance window, because it's simple and simple things fail less. Site Recovery only for rehosting a whole VM — it's not a database migration tool and I'd push back on anyone proposing it for one.

What breaks after a SQL Server → Managed Instance migration that people forget?

The things that live outside the database backup. Logins are server-scoped, so the users inside the restored database are orphaned until the SIDs are remapped. SQL Agent jobs aren't in the backup — they need scripting across separately, and the job steps often reference paths or proxies that don't exist on MI. Linked servers, credentials, server-level triggers, database mail profiles, same story. TDE certificates have to be in place before the restore or it won't attach. And the one that causes the post-go-live incident: the compatibility level and cardinality estimator change, so a handful of queries regress — which is why I have Query Store on before the cutover with a baseline captured, so I can force the old plan while I fix the root cause. Collation is the one you cannot fix afterwards, because MI's instance collation is set at creation.

How do you size the Azure target for an on-prem SQL Server?

Not from the current VM spec, which is almost always a decade-old guess with fear baked in. I run the Azure Migrate SQL assessment, which collects actual CPU, memory and IOPS from the source over a period — I want at least a full business cycle, so month-end is captured — and it recommends a tier and size with a monthly cost, with Hybrid Benefit applied if we have Software Assurance. I cross-check the IO numbers against perfmon myself, because a workload that's been IO-starved on old storage will look small and then wake up. Then I deliberately plan to re-size after 30 days on real telemetry, and I tell the business that up front so the first bill isn't a surprise and the right-sizing isn't seen as a mistake.

Interview Q&A

TDE with a service-managed key or a customer-managed key?

Service-managed is on by default and covers the "data at rest is encrypted" requirement with zero operational burden — for most workloads that's genuinely the right answer and I'd say so rather than gold-plating. Customer-managed keys from Key Vault matter when there's a control requirement: the organisation must be able to prove it controls the key, needs its own rotation schedule, or needs the ability to revoke access to the data independently of Microsoft — the crypto-shredding story. The cost is real operational responsibility: purge protection on the vault, the server's managed identity keeping access, and an understanding that deleting or losing that key makes the database unrecoverable. So I'd ask what the actual requirement is rather than assume CMK is automatically better.

Always Encrypted versus TDE — when does each apply?

They protect against different attackers, so it's not either/or. TDE encrypts the files at rest, which protects against someone taking the storage or a backup — but a sysadmin querying the database sees plaintext, because SQL Server decrypts it. Always Encrypted encrypts specific columns in the client driver, so the keys never reach the database engine and even a compromised sysadmin sees ciphertext. That's the control when the threat model includes privileged insiders or when a regulator asks who can see cardholder data. The trade-off is real: equality-only comparisons unless you use secure enclaves, no LIKE or range queries on those columns, application changes, and key management the application team has to take seriously. I'd use TDE everywhere and Always Encrypted on the specific columns that justify the pain.

How do you remove passwords from your Azure database estate?

Entra-only authentication on the servers, which disables SQL logins entirely so there's nothing to leak. The server admin is an Entra group, not a person, so joiners and leavers are handled by group membership rather than by remembering. Applications authenticate with a managed identity — I create a contained user from the external provider and grant role membership, and the platform handles the token. Anything genuinely needing a secret goes in Key Vault with rotation, accessed by managed identity, so the secret exists in exactly one place. And PIM for the break-glass path, so nobody holds standing admin. The end state is that there is no password in a config file anywhere, and the access review is a list of group memberships rather than an archaeology project.

An auditor asks you to prove every production database was encrypted for the past year. Show me.

Azure Policy compliance state, with the "SQL databases should have transparent data encryption enabled" policy assigned at the management group scope — that gives a per-resource compliance history rather than a point-in-time screenshot. Alongside it, Defender for Cloud's regulatory compliance dashboard maps the same evidence onto the specific control the auditor is citing, which saves an argument about interpretation. The Activity Log shows any change to the encryption setting and who made it. And because the policy has a Deny effect rather than just Audit, I can also demonstrate that a non-compliant database couldn't have been created in the first place — which is a stronger statement than "we checked and they're all fine".

Interview Q&A

Design monitoring for 30 Azure SQL databases.

One Log Analytics workspace as the single pane, with diagnostic settings pushed by an Azure Policy DeployIfNotExists at the management group — so the thirty databases are covered and so is the thirty-first that someone creates next month, which is the part manual configuration always misses. Collect errors, blocks, deadlocks, timeouts, Query Store runtime stats and audit events, with a retention split: 30 days hot for troubleshooting, then archive to storage for the compliance period, because full retention in the workspace gets expensive fast. Alerts: CPU and storage against thresholds, but with dynamic thresholds for anything seasonal, plus a composite condition so on-call gets paged for something real. A workbook as the daily dashboard, and Defender for SQL on for the security half.

Where does an Azure database bill actually go?

Compute first — the vCores are running whether or not anyone queries them, so idle non-production is the biggest chunk of waste in most estates. Then licensing, which is folded into the price unless Hybrid Benefit is applied, and that's a decision someone has to actively make; I've seen estates paying full price with a cupboard full of Software Assurance. Then storage, which only ever grows, plus backup storage that people forget is billed separately once you exceed the included allocation — long-term retention on a big database is a real line. Then egress, particularly geo-replication traffic to another region. My first pass on any inherited estate is: Hybrid Benefit on, dev/test auto-paused, backups lifecycled, then right-size on telemetry, and only then reservations — because reserving the wrong size locks in the mistake for three years.

Give me a KQL query you actually use.

The one I run most is the errors query — filter AzureDiagnostics to the Errors category with severity 17 and above, project the timestamp, resource and message, order descending. That's my "what actually broke overnight" across the whole estate in one query rather than thirty error logs. The second is failed logins from SQLSecurityAuditEvents summarised by principal and client IP, which catches both an application with a stale password and an actual brute-force attempt, and it's the thing the security team asks for. And a Query Store runtime stats query summing duration by query hash for the top offenders, which is how I find the regression after a compat level change without logging into each database.

Interview Q&A

Design HA/DR for a business-critical Azure SQL estate.

In-region: Business Critical tier with zone redundancy, so a data-hall failure is handled in seconds with zero data loss and I get a readable secondary out of it for reporting. Cross-region: an auto-failover group to the paired region, which gives me an RPO under five seconds and, more importantly, a listener endpoint so the application's connection string doesn't change during a failover. PITR at 35 days for operational recovery, long-term retention for the compliance obligation, and geo-redundant backup storage so geo-restore exists as a floor. Then the parts that make it real rather than theoretical: a resource lock on the logical server, because deleting the server deletes the backups, and a scheduled planned failover test where we time it and update the runbook. The automatic failover has an hour's grace period by default, so if the RTO is tighter than that, someone has to make the call — and that person needs a documented decision tree, not a hunch at 3 a.m.

Always On AG on Azure VMs — what's different from on-prem?

Quorum uses a cloud witness in a storage account rather than a file share, which is cheaper and has nothing to maintain. The listener is the big one: historically you needed an internal load balancer with a health probe on a dedicated port, and it was fiddly and slow to fail over. On Windows Server 2016 and later with SQL 2019 and later you use a Distributed Network Name listener instead, which removes the load balancer entirely — faster failover, less to break. Nodes go across availability zones rather than an availability set where the region supports it, and clients need MultiSubnetFailover=True. Everything else — sync versus async, quorum maths, the AG itself — is exactly what I already know from on-prem.

Someone deleted a production database at 09:00. It's 09:20. What do you do?

Restore it — a deleted database on a surviving logical server is restorable within the PITR window, so I restore to a point just before the deletion, into a new database name rather than over anything. While that runs, I pull the Activity Log to establish who and how, because the RCA starts now, not after. Then the application team validates against the restored copy before I rename and cut over, because restoring is easy and restoring the wrong point is expensive. Afterwards: a CanNotDelete lock on the server, RBAC reviewed so the person who did it didn't have Owner they didn't need, and if the deletion was accidental the honest RCA is about why that permission existed rather than about the individual. The nightmare version of this question is if they'd deleted the server, because that takes the backups with it — which is exactly why the lock exists.

Interview Q&A

What's the Azure equivalent of RDS?

There isn't one clean answer, and that's the interesting part. Azure splits it: Azure SQL Database is database-scoped PaaS, and SQL Managed Instance is instance-scoped PaaS with the SQL Agent, cross-database queries and native restore that RDS for SQL Server doesn't give you. So for an existing SQL Server estate, MI is genuinely a better lift-and-shift target than RDS — it's the closest thing to "my instance, someone else's patching". For PostgreSQL and MySQL the mapping is much closer: RDS to Flexible Server, roughly feature-for-feature. Aurora is the one with no equivalent — Hyperscale is the closest architecturally, with its separated page servers and log service, but it's not the same product.

Where do the AWS↔Azure analogies mislead people?

Three places I'd call out. Account-to-subscription: an AWS account is a hard isolation boundary with its own IAM and an SCP ceiling above even root, whereas an Azure subscription is mostly billing and quota — the real boundary is the tenant. IAM-to-Entra: Entra ID is identity, Azure RBAC is authorisation, and they're separate systems, whereas IAM is both, which is why "the Azure IAM policy" is a sentence that means nothing. And SCP-to-Azure Policy: an SCP denies API actions, Policy evaluates resource properties at deployment — so "deny anyone deleting a database" is natural in an SCP and awkward in Policy, where you'd reach for a resource lock instead. Getting those wrong is how a design that worked in one cloud quietly loses its guardrails in the other.

Interview Q&A

Your company runs AWS and just acquired an Azure shop. What's your database strategy?

First, I don't migrate anything on day one — the acquisition already has enough disruption, and a rushed migration during integration is how you lose data and people. I'd standardise the things that hurt most when they're split: identity into one directory so joiners and leavers are one process, one place the on-call sees alerts, one backup and DR evidence report for audit, and Terraform for anything new. Then I'd assess each workload on its merits. If they're an Entra shop with SQL Server, Managed Instance may genuinely be the better home, and moving it to RDS to satisfy a tidiness instinct costs money and gains nothing. Over eighteen months you'd expect consolidation where there's a real case — usually cost or a skills gap on the smaller platform — but "one cloud" as a goal in itself is a slogan, not a strategy. What I would be firm about is not letting the second platform be operated informally: same standards, same reviews, same runbooks, or it becomes the estate nobody understands.

How do you manage 60 SQL Server instances across on-prem, EC2 and Azure VMs?

Azure Arc, honestly, because it's the only thing that gives me one inventory across all three — it projects the on-prem and EC2 instances into Azure Resource Manager, so I get a single query returning every instance with its version and edition, plus Defender for SQL, best-practices assessment and Azure Policy applied to machines that aren't in Azure. That solves the problem that actually bites, which is that nobody knows what they have. Underneath it, standard tooling regardless of location: Ola Hallengren for maintenance, the same health-check toolkit, dbatools and PowerShell for automation, backups landing in object storage with immutability. The point is that the location becomes an attribute of the instance rather than a different operating model.

Is multi-cloud a good idea?

Usually it's a fact rather than a decision — an acquisition, a regulator, a team that went its own way. Where it's genuinely chosen, the honest cases are provider concentration risk for a regulated firm, or one platform having a service that materially wins for a specific workload. The case I'd push back on is avoiding lock-in, because the way people implement that is to use only the services both clouds share, which means running your own everything and paying for the privilege — you've swapped vendor lock-in for the operational burden the vendor was absorbing. My position: be excellent on one, deliberate about the second, standardise identity and observability across both, and let the databases use each platform's best service rather than forcing a common denominator.

Interview Q&A

Production database is down. Walk me through your first ten minutes.

Establish impact first — who's affected and since when — because that decides everything else, including whether I wake people up. Declare the incident and open the channel; naming it early is cheap and un-declaring is easy. Then evidence before action: is the platform telling me it's degraded? Resource Health or the Personal Health Dashboard is scoped to my resources and often knows before I do. Did anything change — a deploy, a maintenance window, a config edit? The Activity Log or CloudTrail answers that in seconds. What do the metrics say — CPU, storage, connections, failover events? Then a first stakeholder update at around eight minutes even if it says nothing more than "investigating, next update in twenty". And then I mitigate rather than debug: if failing over restores service, I fail over and diagnose afterwards from the evidence I collected. What I don't do is restart the instance to see if it helps, because that destroys what I need for the RCA and usually doesn't fix it.

Tell me about a time you caused an outage.

The shape of a good answer: name it plainly, no drama. Say what you did, what broke, and how long. Say what you did in the moment — did you own up immediately? Then the important half: what you found in the RCA that wasn't about you. An index rebuild that blocked production because it ran without a resumable option and outside the window is a story about a missing guardrail as much as about a mistake — the fix is a change process and a maintenance window that's enforced, not "I'll be more careful". Close with the actual outcome: what changed afterwards, and whether it happened again. Interviewers are listening for ownership without self-flagellation and for whether you think in systems.

An application team insists the database is the problem. It isn't. How do you handle it?

I don't argue, I share evidence — and I'd rather do that in the same room than over a ticket. I'd show what the database is actually doing during their slow window: query duration from Query Store or Performance Insights, waits, the fact that the same query is completing in eight milliseconds server-side. If it's ASYNC_NETWORK_IO or the client-side timing doesn't match the server-side timing, that points at the application consuming results slowly or a connection pool problem, and I'd say that as a hypothesis rather than a verdict. The tone matters: they're not wrong to look at the database first, it's the shared dependency and it's what everyone blames. What ends these well is offering to look together — I bring the server-side timings, they bring the client-side ones, and the gap between them is the answer. Being right and unpleasant about it means the next incident takes longer, because they'll stop calling.

How do you decide when to wake someone up?

Business impact, not technical severity. A failed backup on a dev instance can wait until morning; a production database at 95% storage cannot, because the trajectory means it becomes an outage regardless of the hour. My rule is: if the situation will get materially worse before business hours, or if it's already affecting revenue or customers, it's a page. If it's stable and diagnosable in daylight, it's a ticket. And that has to be codified in the alerting rather than left to judgement at 3 a.m., because judgement at 3 a.m. is exactly the thing that's degraded — which is also why I care about alert quality. If on-call gets paged for things that don't need them, they'll start ignoring the one that did.

The scenarioA retail group runs 60 SQL Server instances on-prem: 2 business-critical (24×7 POS and inventory, 4 TB and 2 TB), 12 important (warehouse, finance — finance is SOX-scoped), 46 small (reporting, departmental, several on unsupported versions). The board wants "cloud" in 18 months. You're asked to present a plan in the final interview round. This is one story that lets you demonstrate every chapter.

Phase 1 — Discover (weeks 1–6)

Interview Q&A

Present your migration plan in two minutes.

Six weeks of discovery, because I refuse to plan on a wrong inventory — appliance-based discovery, DMA for blockers, thirty days of telemetry including a month-end, and every database mapped to an owner. Then decide per application, not per estate: Managed Instance is the default because it keeps Agent jobs and cross-database queries so the app doesn't change; SQL Database in elastic pools for the long tail of small ones, which is where the cost saving actually lives; VM only where a blocker forces it, documented as an exception. The unowned databases get retired, and that's typically a quarter of the estate. We land the platform in parallel — management groups, Policy denying public endpoints, private endpoints, Entra-only auth, everything in Terraform. Migration order is deliberately small-and-boring first to prove the runbook, business-critical last via MI link once the team has done it a dozen times. Then optimise on real telemetry before buying reservations, and prove it with a timed failover drill and a restore test. First production database live at month four, done at eighteen.

The board wants it done in 6 months, not 18. What do you say?

I'd ask what's driving the date, because the answer changes the response entirely — a data centre lease expiring is a real constraint I can plan around, and a slide with "cloud" on it is not. Then I'd be specific rather than defensive: here's what fits in six months. The 46 small databases and the unowned retirement can genuinely be done, and that's the majority of the instance count and a real chunk of the cost. What doesn't fit is the business-critical pair, because the constraint isn't my effort, it's the application teams' capacity to test, and the risk of a rushed cutover on POS at Christmas is a revenue event that dwarfs the saving. So the counter-proposal is: 80% of the instances in six months, the crown jewels on a schedule that respects the testing, and a monthly readout so nobody's surprised. What I won't do is agree to a date I can't hit — that just moves the bad news to a point where it's more expensive.

Halfway through, an application team says their database can't move. What now?

First, I find out whether "can't" is technical or organisational, because the responses are completely different. If it's technical — a genuine blocker, a vendor who won't support the platform, a driver that doesn't exist — then that's a decision for the exception log with an owner and a review date, and it goes on a VM or stays where it is. That's fine; a migration with no exceptions is a migration that hasn't met reality. If it's organisational — no capacity to test, no confidence, a bad experience in a previous programme — then it's not my problem to solve alone and pushing harder makes it worse. I'd take it to the steering group with the actual cost of the exception attached, because "we keep a data centre open for one database" is a number, and numbers move conversations that arguments don't.
